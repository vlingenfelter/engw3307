After using Blackboard and Canvas, we’ve learned these 3 powerful lessons for eLearning platforms

In the past year, schools everywhere have been leaning on eLearning platforms as the primary means of managing the academic experience. The ‘academic experience’ consists of all the interactions that facilitate learning. Some of these interactions discussed here include peer to peer interactions, professors interacting with their students, students interacting with their coursework, and communication among the students. In these chaotic times, schools scrambled to adopt whatever platform they could as quickly as they could. But not all eLearning platforms are created equally. In this post, we will draw on our experiences with Blackboard and Canvas, two learning management systems used by Northeastern University, to develop insights about eLearning platforms at large.

1. User Experience matters… a lot.

Canvas is just pretty. One of the biggest appeals of Canvas is its user friendly features. When users first log into Canvas, there is less clutter on the home screen and its layout is much smoother than Blackboard. When users first log into Blackboard, all of the functions are listed on one page without a means to organize or hide unnecessary categories. Not only does Canvas organize the various components of its platform in a visually appealing manner, it also gives users the options to add or delete features. Users can choose between various color themes for the platform, and instead of listing everything, Canvas uses Google-inspired material design cards to organize classes.

Canvas uses Google-inspired material design cards to organize classes

With Canvas, each class can have customized images and symbols to represent the work performed by both students and professors. Blackboard does not have any of these features, only presenting a similar monotone experience for all classes. In a world where all of school is online, the beauty and efficiency of design for eLearning platforms matters. A good UI can take an unfortunate situation and elevate it to something functional and enjoyable.

2. Not all eLearning platforms are suited to completely virtual learning

Canvas is also better suited to maintaining the academic experience, even in a learning environment that has moved to completely virtual. Just think about the messaging feature. Blackboard only provides students with contact information for professors, and this is only available if the professors decide to add their information to their profile. Oftentimes, many professors chose not to do this and simply provided their contact information at the beginning of the course.

Canvas, however, provides students with a direct means of communicating with the professors and each other, and a way to represent themselves virtually. Canvas lets students and professors customize their profiles by adding avatars, biographies, links, and social media accounts. Messages are delivered to the intended recipient's Canvas inbox. Canvas also provides a list of all members in a class, which allows for easy communication between students.

Canvas lets students and professors customize their profiles by adding avatars, biographies, links, and social media accounts

3. Instructor/student relationships are not one way streets

Blackboard focuses on delivering the message from the instructors to the students. The Homepage of Blackboard centers the Announcements section, where students are given notifications, posts, and messages from professors. In each class section, the students are presented To Do lists (tasks that are due soon on the front page), which further show the direct instruction from the professor. Blackboard primarily has one way communication from professors to students. The professors hold the more powerful tools, and are able to direct the students without their input or feedback. Blackboard limits student-to-student communication as well, stifling learning community growth.

Blackboard’s Home Page shows the one-way communication from Professor through the means of Announcements, Tasks, To Do list and Alerts

On Canvas, instructors can divide up the class into groups for group work, and the integrated messaging platform allows groups to contact each other immediately and seamlessly. Each group has a section of Canvas automatically generated for it, which allows group members to share files, start discussion boards, and contact each other. This feature saves time and eliminates contacting the wrong individual. It also stimulates a collaborative environment and mimics more closely to the real life experience of meeting your classmates in class.

Each group has a section of Canvas automatically generated for it, which allows group members to share files, start discussion boards, and contact each other.

Conclusion

Since E-learning platforms have become such an integral part of the education system, it is important to understand the values they give to the users. From analyzing the functions of Canvas and Blackboard, it is evident that each delivers their users a different experience, study environment and distinct discourse community. Blackboard, in general, specifically focuses on delivering the message from the teachers by giving instructors more tools, means of communication. Its straightforward approach proved effective during its time of use because it was used in conjunction with in person learning. Therefore, any gaps within the translation of information could be easily mended by face to face interactions. Canvas creates a more accessible discourse between all members of a classroom. By allowing students to readily interact with each other, Canvas helps bridge the gap between virtual and in-person learning. Ultimately, Canvas provides a more holistic and rich experience for students, which is especially important in these isolating times.
